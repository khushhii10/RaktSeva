Frequently Asked Questions

What is blood donation?

+

Blood donation is the voluntary act of giving blood to help those in need. It is a lifesaving procedure that can help save patients suffering from accidents, surgeries, and diseases.

Who can donate blood?

+

Generally, healthy individuals between the ages of 18 and 65, weighing at least 50kg, and not suffering from any contagious diseases can donate blood. Please consult with a doctor for more specific criteria.

How often can I donate blood?

+

Typically, whole blood can be donated once every 56 days, plasma can be donated more frequently, and platelets can be donated every 7 days. Please consult your healthcare provider for personalized recommendations.

Is donating blood safe?

+

Yes, donating blood is safe. Blood donation procedures are strictly regulated and carried out using sterile, single-use equipment to eliminate any risk of infection. Health professionals carefully assess your suitability before each donation, and blood is thoroughly tested for diseases like HIV, hepatitis, and syphilis. While some people may feel light-headed or experience minor discomfort, these symptoms are typically short-lived and rarely lead to serious complications.

What happens to the blood after donation?

+

After blood is collected, it is sent to a blood bank or hospital where it is tested for any infections or diseases. Once cleared, the blood is separated into its components (red blood cells, plasma, and platelets). These components are then stored in specific conditions and used for patients in need. Red blood cells are used for surgeries, trauma patients, and anemia; plasma is used for burn victims and patients with clotting disorders; and platelets are used in cancer treatments and for patients undergoing chemotherapy.
